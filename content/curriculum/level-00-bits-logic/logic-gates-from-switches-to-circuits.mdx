Boolean logic, as you just learned it, lives on paper — it's math. This lesson makes it physical. A <b>logic gate</b> is a real electronic circuit, built from transistors, that computes AND, OR, or NOT using actual current instead of symbols. It's the same two-state, high-or-low idea from lesson one, wired together to compute something.

By the end you'll be able to explain how switches wired together produce AND/OR behaviour, recognize the standard gate symbols, understand why a single gate type (NAND) is enough to build all the others, and trace how a couple of gates combine to actually add two bits — your first real glimpse of computing hardware doing arithmetic.

## Boolean algebra, wired up

Early computers built logic out of electromechanical relays — physical switches flipped by an electromagnet. Modern chips use transistors instead, but the idea is identical: a transistor is an electrically controlled switch, current either flows or it doesn't, exactly the two states from lesson one. A logic gate is simply a small arrangement of these switches wired to compute one boolean operator.

Wire two switches in <i>series</i> (one after another, along the same path) and a bulb at the end only lights when <i>both</i> switches are closed — that's AND. Wire the same two switches in <i>parallel</i> (two separate paths to the same bulb) and the bulb lights if <i>either</i> switch is closed — that's OR. NOT is even simpler: a switch that lets current through when open and blocks it when closed, inverting the input.

## The gate zoo, and the one that builds all the others

Engineering diagrams use standard symbols for each gate: a D-shaped symbol for AND, a curved shield shape for OR, a triangle with a small bubble for NOT. `NAND` is just AND followed by NOT — the bubble on an AND symbol — and it flips the AND truth table exactly.

Here's the remarkable fact: NAND alone is enough to build AND, OR, NOT, XOR — every boolean function that exists — by wiring NAND gates to each other in the right combinations. That's why it's called a <b>universal gate</b>, and why real chips are, underneath, mostly the same few gate types stamped out by the billions rather than a different physical component for every operator.

## Gates that add: the half-adder

Put an XOR gate and an AND gate side by side, both fed the same two input bits A and B, and you've built a <b>half-adder</b>: the XOR output is the <i>sum</i> bit (0+0=0, 0+1=1, 1+1=0 with a carry), and the AND output is the <i>carry</i> bit (only true when both inputs are 1, exactly when a carry is needed).

This is the seed of every arithmetic operation a computer performs. Chain more of these together with a bit of extra logic for carrying between columns, and you get a circuit that adds numbers of any size — built entirely from the handful of gates you just learned. That circuit, and others like it, are what the next lesson puts to work inside the loop that makes a CPU run.

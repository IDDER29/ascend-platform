Everything your program reads from or writes to — the terminal, a file, a pipe, a network socket — is represented by the exact same kind of handle: a small integer called a <b>file descriptor</b>. By convention, every process is born with three already open: `0` (stdin), `1` (stdout), and `2` (stderr).

This lesson has one job: show you that redirection isn't shell magic, it's three ordinary syscalls — `open()`, `dup2()`, and `close()`. This is exactly what your shell will do with `>` and `<` — once this clicks, you're ready to build Shellforge.

## Everything is a file descriptor

Each process keeps its own small table mapping integers to open files — fd 0, 1, and 2 point at the controlling terminal by default, and every `open()` call hands back the next free number. Reading from stdin, writing to a log file, and sending bytes down a pipe are all, from your code's perspective, just `read(fd, ...)` and `write(fd, ...)` on some integer.

This table survives `fork()` (the child gets its own copy, same numbers pointing at the same open files) and, crucially, it survives `exec()` too — which is the whole trick behind redirection.

## Redirecting with dup2()

`dup2(newfd, oldfd)` makes `oldfd` point at whatever `newfd` currently points at — silently closing whatever oldfd used to point at first. Call `dup2(fd, STDOUT_FILENO)` and every future write to fd 1, from <i>anywhere</i> in the program including libraries you didn't write, ends up wherever fd pointed.

The pattern below — open a file, dup2 it onto a standard fd, close the now-redundant extra descriptor — is the entire redirection trick. Run it and nothing prints to your terminal at all; it all lands in `output.txt` instead.

<CodeBlock lang='c' linesJson='["#include &lt;fcntl.h&gt;", "#include &lt;stdio.h&gt;", "#include &lt;unistd.h&gt;", "", "int main(void) {", "    int fd = open(\"output.txt\", O_WRONLY | O_CREAT | O_TRUNC, 0644);", "", "    dup2(fd, STDOUT_FILENO);   /* fd 1 now points where fd points */", "    close(fd);                 /* don&#39;t need the extra fd anymore */", "", "    printf(\"this line goes to the file, not the terminal\\n\");", "", "    return 0;", "}"]' runnable expectedOutput='(nothing prints to the terminal — output.txt now contains: "this line goes to the file, not the terminal")' />

## This is exactly what > and < do

When your shell parses `ls > out.txt`, it forks a child, and in that child — <i>before</i> calling `execve()` — it does exactly the open/dup2/close sequence above. Because the fd table survives exec, ls itself never knows anything was redirected; it just calls `printf()` like always, and the bytes end up in the file. `<` is the identical trick in reverse, opening a file read-only and dup2'ing it onto fd 0.

Put this together with the pipe lesson and you have the whole shell: `|` is two children dup2'ing pipe ends onto their stdin/stdout instead of files. You now have every piece — fork, wait, syscalls, pipes, signals, and this — needed to build Shellforge. Go build it.
